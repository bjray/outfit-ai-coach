## Overview

The Uphill Athlete methodology is a structured, science-based approach to training for mountain sports. It builds a massive aerobic base through high-volume, low-intensity work, then layers sport-specific intensity and strength on top. Its central claim is that mountain athletes — alpinists, mountaineers, trail runners, ski mountaineers, and climbers — are fundamentally **endurance athletes**, and that durable mountain performance comes from building the underlying aerobic engine before pursuing intensity.

Canonical book references:

- *Training for the New Alpinism* (House & Johnston) — alpinism, mountaineering, ice/mixed climbing
- *Training for the Uphill Athlete* (House, Johnston & Jornet) — trail running, ski mountaineering, general mountain fitness

This protocol file is synthesized from the uphillathlete.com article corpus (snapshot in `inbox/uphill-athlete/`). Every numeric prescription below is traceable to a specific source article; content not substantiated in that snapshot is marked `(inferred)`.

## Core Principles

1. **Aerobic base dominates — 80-90% of training volume in Zone 1-2.** High-intensity work is strategic, limited to 10-20% of weekly volume. If you can't hold a conversation, you're going too hard.
2. **Build capacity before utilization.** Early training builds the physiological engine (aerobic base, max strength, muscular endurance). Later training maps that capacity onto event-specific demands.
3. **Three training systems, distinct not interchangeable.** Endurance, Stamina, and Work Capacity each have different prescriptions and periodization. Training all three equally simultaneously reduces effectiveness.
4. **Strength is supplemental, not primary.** 2x/week in the gym supports the mountain — it does not replace aerobic work and must not leave you fatigued for it.
5. **Recovery is an active training variable.** Sleep (7-9 hrs), post-training nutrition, hydration, and planned deload weeks (every 3-4 weeks, 30-50% volume reduction) are load-bearing prescriptions, not optional hygiene.
6. **Individualize via testing, not guessing.** The HR Drift Test establishes each athlete's Aerobic Threshold (AeT) and defines Zone 2 personally; use real data, not age-based formulas.

## The Three Training Systems

Mountain athletes train three distinct systems. Each has its own dosage and periodization emphasis.

### Endurance

- **Prescription:** 4-5x/week Zone 1-2 aerobic, 60-180+ min, conversational pace.
- **Role:** The backbone. Builds mitochondrial density, capillary networks, fat oxidation.
- **Phase emphasis:** Dominates Base Phase; sustained across all phases.

### Stamina

- **Prescription:** 2-3x/week weighted step-ups, **3-4 sets of 15-20 reps per leg, 15-30% body weight pack**.
- **Role:** Muscular endurance for sustained load-bearing under fatigue.
- **Phase emphasis:** Ramps during Build; peaks in Peak Phase.

### Work Capacity

- **Prescription:** 1-2x/week hill sprints, heavy carries, or strength-endurance circuits (example: 5 rounds of 60s carries + 30s step-ups + 30s rest).
- **Role:** Tolerance for high-intensity, sustained output — the top end.
- **Phase emphasis:** Low in Base; introduced in Build; sport-specific in Peak.

**Off-season** is 80-90% Endurance with minimal Stamina and Work Capacity. **Pre-season** increases Stamina. **Peak (4-6 weeks before objective)** shifts to event-specific Work Capacity.

> **Source:** Training_Endurance_Stamina_Work_Capacity_Mountain_Athletes.md, Muscular_Endurance_Alpinists_Mountaineers.md.

## Capacity vs. Utilization

A second lens over the same phases:

- **Capacity training** builds the physiological engine: Zone 1-2 aerobic (4-5x/week), max-strength (2x/week, low-rep/high-weight), muscular endurance (1-2x/week).
- **Utilization training** teaches the body to use that engine on sport-specific demand: Zone 3-4 tempo (1x/week), steep terrain with load, extended back-to-back efforts.

Phase mapping:

- **Base Phase:** 80-90% capacity, minimal utilization.
- **Build Phase:** Shift toward utilization; keep capacity work but reduce volume slightly.
- **Peak Phase (4-6 weeks):** Utilization dominates; volume reduces; intensity maintained or climbs.

> **Source:** Capacity_vs_Utilization_Training_in_Endurance_Training.md.

## Heart Rate Zones

Zones are defined by **physiological anchors**, not by fabricated BPM math. The two anchors are Aerobic Threshold (AeT) and Anaerobic Threshold (AnT). Determine AeT via the HR Drift Test (below). AnT can be estimated from a ~30-min all-out time trial (inferred — no AnT test protocol was included in the source snapshot; verify against the books).

| Zone | Name | Intensity anchor | Experience |
|------|------|------------------|------------|
| 1 | Recovery | Well below AeT | Very easy; can carry on full conversation; nose breathing all day |
| 2 | Aerobic Base | At or just below AeT | Conversational pace; nose-breathing mostly possible. **The primary training zone.** |
| 3 | Tempo / Threshold | Between AeT and AnT | "Comfortably hard." Used sparingly; junk-miles risk if overused |
| 4 | VO2max / Intervals | At or above AnT | Hard intervals; reserved for Build/Peak phases and experienced athletes |
| 5 | Sprint / Neuromuscular | Maximal, very short | Sprints, explosive work |

Distribution target across a week: **80-90% Zone 1-2, 10-20% Zone 3-5**. Never let total high-intensity work exceed 20% of weekly volume.

> **Sources:** Core_Principles_of_Endurance_Training.md, The_Science_Physiology_of_Endurance_Training.md, Endurance_Training_Summary.md. The Zone 5 row and the AnT 30-min time trial are (inferred) — not explicit in the snapshot.

## Tests: HR Drift Test for AeT

The Heart Rate Drift Test estimates Aerobic Threshold by measuring cardiac drift at a steady effort.

**Setup:**
- Use a **chest-strap** HR monitor. Wrist-based is not accurate enough.
- Treadmill or flat ground. Avoid uneven terrain.
- Fed, hydrated, not overheated.

**Protocol:**
1. **Warm-up:** 15-20 min in Zone 1, HR stable.
2. **Test:** 60 minutes at a steady, consistent effort (pace or power held constant).
3. **Analyze:** Compare average HR of the first 30 min against the last 30 min.

**Interpretation:**
- **HR drift <5%** → you were below AeT. AeT is higher than your test HR; retest at a slightly harder effort.
- **HR drift 5-10%** → you were at or near AeT. The test HR estimates your AeT.
- **HR drift >10%** → you were above AeT. Drop the effort and retest.

**Retest frequency:** every 4-6 weeks to track adaptation.

> **Source:** How_to_Perform_Heart_Rate_Drift_Test_AeT.md.

## Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome (ADS)

**Concept:** An athlete with a large gap between AeT and AnT is "aerobically deficient" — they have a high top end relative to a weak aerobic base. Symptoms: HR shoots up quickly under effort, steady Zone 2 feels boring or forced, long low-intensity sessions feel harder than short hard ones.

It may be in *Training for the New Alpinism* or *Training for the Uphill Athlete*; verify against the books and update the Changelog when confirmed.

**Practical guidance even without the formula:** if the HR Drift Test shows a stubbornly low AeT, if Zone 2 efforts produce disproportionate fatigue, or if the athlete has been training at high intensity for years without building base, spend **8-16 weeks in pure Zone 1-2 base building** before adding any Zone 3-4 work.

## Phase Structure

Five phases, not four. Order: Transition → Base → Build → Peak → Taper. A full cycle for an objective is typically 16-24 weeks.

### Transition Period (~8 weeks)

The opening phase — rebuild after a break, establish movement quality, begin linear strength progression.

**Volume start:** **50% of the athlete's maximum weekly training volume.** Example: if max is 600 min/week, start at 300 min/week.

**Allocation:** ~60% strength, ~40% conditioning.

**Two-week block structure** (repeat for ~4 blocks = 8 weeks):
- 6 strength-specific sessions (3 per week)
- 1 ruck with **10-15% bodyweight** (intentionally light)
- 1 aerobic interval day (long intervals, short rests)
- 1 structural recovery day
- 1 gym stamina day (sustained work capacity)
- 1 active recovery day (hiking, sports, etc.)

**Progressive loading schedule** (applied to weekly volume):
- Weeks 1-3: Maintain baseline (e.g., 300 min/wk).
- Week 4: +15%.
- Week 5: Maintain Week 4.
- Week 6: +10% from Weeks 4-5.
- Week 7: Maintain Week 6.
- Week 8: +5% from Weeks 6-7.
- (If extending to 12 weeks: Week 9 maintain; Weeks 10-12 +5% from Weeks 8-9.)

**Strength progression:** Linear, **3×5 sets/reps**, adding **5-10 lbs per session** across the big barbell lifts (squat, bench, overhead press, deadlift, power clean).

**Exit criteria:** End the Transition Period when linear strength progression stalls. A second deload within the phase often signals it's time to move to Base.

> **Source:** transition-period-knowledge-base.md, Transition_Period_for_Mountain_Athletes.md.

### Base Period (3-4 months)

The longest and most important phase. Build the aerobic engine and maintain/develop max strength.

- **Volume:** Progressive, 10-15% per week, with a recovery week every 3-4 weeks (30-50% reduction).
- **Intensity:** 90-100% Zone 1-2. No Zone 3-4 work — especially if ADS is present.
- **Modality:** Specific to the objective (hiking, easy running, uphill treadmill, ski touring, ski mountaineering).
- **Strength:** 2x/week max strength (see Strength section).
- **Vertical:** Accumulate vertical gain progressively — the single most sport-specific metric for mountain athletes.

**Weekly structure example** (intermediate, ~8-10 hrs/week):

```
Mon: Rest or easy Zone 1 (30-45 min)
Tue: Gym max-strength (45-60 min) + Zone 2 easy aerobic (30-45 min)
Wed: Zone 2 long aerobic (60-90 min) with vertical gain
Thu: Gym max-strength (45-60 min) + Zone 1 recovery (30 min)
Fri: Rest
Sat: Long Zone 1-2 (2-4 hrs) with significant vertical gain
Sun: Zone 1-2 moderate (60-90 min)
```

> **Sources:** Aerobic_Endurance_Periodization.md, What_Enables_Endurance_Key_Physiological_Adaptations.md, Core_Principles_of_Endurance_Training.md.

### Build Period (6-8 weeks)

Introduce utilization work while keeping the base intact.

- **Volume:** Maintain or slightly reduce.
- **Intensity:** Introduce **1 Zone 3 session per week** first; add a second if tolerated. Total high-intensity work still ≤ 20% of weekly volume.
- **Strength:** Shift one of the two weekly strength days toward **muscular endurance** (see Strength section).
- **Key workouts:**
  - Tempo intervals at Zone 3 (e.g., 3-4 × 10 min at AeT → AnT, with 3 min Zone 1 recovery)
  - Long days with objective-specific demands (extended loaded carry, long steep vert)

> **Sources:** Capacity_vs_Utilization_Training_in_Endurance_Training.md, Aerobic_Endurance_Periodization.md, What_Enables_Endurance_Key_Physiological_Adaptations.md.

### Peak Period (4-6 weeks pre-objective)

Utilization dominates. Volume reduces; intensity maintained or increases.

- **Volume:** Step down gradually from Build levels.
- **Specificity:** Training closely mimics the objective (loaded vert, route-rehearsal pace, event-day fueling).
- **Stamina work:** Peak dose of weighted step-ups (3-4 sets × 15-20 reps, 15-30% BW) 2-3x/week.
- **Work capacity:** 1-2x/week sport-specific high-intensity (hill sprints, heavy carries, circuits).
- **Zone 4 use:** Max 1x/week; only for experienced athletes who have handled it in earlier phases.

> **Source:** Aerobic_Endurance_Periodization.md, Capacity_vs_Utilization_Training_in_Endurance_Training.md, Muscular_Endurance_Alpinists_Mountaineers.md.

### Taper (2 weeks)

The final lead-in before the objective.

- **Volume:** Reduce by **40-60%**.
- **Intensity:** Maintain in 1-2 short, sharp workouts; otherwise easy.
- **Recovery:** Sleep, nutrition, hydration prioritized over all else.
- **Final week:** Very easy, mostly rest, light movement.

> **Source:** Transition_Period_for_Mountain_Athletes.md (treats recovery/transition analogously); taper specifics (inferred) from standard periodization — not separately documented in the snapshot.

## Strength Training

Strength is supplemental: 2x/week year-round, with load adjusted by phase.

### Max Strength (Base Phase emphasis)

- **Pattern:** 4-5 sets of **3-5 reps at 85-90% 1RM**; 3-5 min rest between sets.
- **Lifts (pick 4, rotate):** Deadlift, squat, weighted step-ups, weighted pull-ups, overhead press, rows.
- **Frequency:** 2x/week.
- **Golden rule:** Strength work must **not** leave you too sore or fatigued for your aerobic sessions. If it does, cut a set.

**KIS (Keep It Simple) template** — 4 movement patterns, 2x/week, year-round:
- **Lower:** Weighted step-ups 3-5 × 4-6 reps/leg at 85-90% 1RM; deadlifts 3-5 × 3-5 reps.
- **Upper pull:** Pull-ups or weighted pull-ups 3-4 × 5-8 reps.
- **Upper push:** Dips or overhead press 3-4 × 5-8 reps.
- **Core:** Planks, hanging leg raises, Russian twists, 2-3 sets of 30-60s.
- **Rest:** 3-5 min between heavy sets.

> **Source:** Strength_Training_Mountain_Athletes_Key_Principles.md, KIS_Strength_for_Mountain_Athletes.md.

### Muscular Endurance (Build & Peak Phase emphasis)

Convert max strength into sustained load capacity.

- **Weighted step-ups (2-3x/week):** **3-4 sets of 15-20 reps per leg, 15-30% body weight pack.** Controlled movement, no bouncing.
- **Hill climbs with load (1-2x/week):** **15-25% body weight, 30-90 min in Zone 1-2.** Long, steady, loaded.
- **Isometric holds (2-3x/week):** Wall sits 1-2 min × 3; planks 3-4 × 30-60s.
- **Progression:** Increase weight **or** duration, not both simultaneously.

> **Source:** Muscular_Endurance_Alpinists_Mountaineers.md.

### Core (all phases)

- **Anti-extension:** planks, dead bugs, ab wheel.
- **Anti-rotation:** pallof press, bird dogs.
- **Hip stability:** clamshells, side-lying leg raises, single-leg balance.
- **Dosage:** 10-15 min after every session, or as a standalone block 3x/week.

> **Source:** Strength_Training_Mountain_Athletes_Key_Principles.md (core stability 3x/week); specific exercise list (inferred) from standard mountain-athlete practice.

## Recovery

Recovery is load-bearing, not optional.

- **Sleep:** 7-9 hours nightly, consistent schedule.
- **Post-training nutrition:** 20-30g protein + carbs within **30-60 min** after training.
- **Hydration:** roughly half body weight (lb) in ounces of water daily; electrolytes in heat or at altitude.
- **Active recovery:** 1-2x/week Zone 1 activities, foam rolling, mobility.
- **Deload weeks:** every **3-4 weeks**, reduce volume **30-50%**.
- **Monitoring:** RHR (resting heart rate), HRV (heart-rate variability), sleep quality, subjective soreness. Sustained RHR rise or HRV drop is an overtraining signal; back off.

> **Source:** Recovery_Strategies_for_Endurance_Athletes.md.

## Progression Rules

**Volume:**
- Increase total weekly hours by **10-15% per week** during build phases.
- Recovery week every **3-4 weeks**, reduce volume **30-50%**.
- Track vertical gain separately from total hours; increase it gradually.

**Intensity:**
- Base Period: no intensity work. All Zone 1-2.
- Build Period: add 1 Zone 3 session/week first, then a second if tolerated.
- Zone 4: Build/Peak only, max 1x/week, experienced athletes.
- Total weekly high-intensity work: never exceed **20% of volume**.

**Strength:**
- Max strength: add weight when all sets completed with clean form.
- Muscular endurance: add rounds or reps first, then add weight to circuits.

> **Sources:** Core_Principles_of_Endurance_Training.md, The_Science_Physiology_of_Endurance_Training.md, Aerobic_Endurance_Periodization.md.

## Sport-Specific Adaptations

### Mountaineering

- Emphasize loaded carries and vertical gain in Base and Build.
- Long weekend sessions with a heavy pack (scale weight to expedition kit).
- Practice with full kit: boots, pack, layers.
- Altitude simulation (masks, altitude tents) if available — (inferred; not in snapshot).

> **Source:** Aerobic_Endurance_Periodization.md.

### Trail Running

- Running-specific volume (vs. hiking-dominant for mountaineering).
- More Zone 3-4 work in Build/Peak than pure mountaineering.
- Downhill running practice for eccentric conditioning.
- Race-specific fueling tested in long sessions.

> **Source:** Training_for_the_Uphill_Athlete_corpus (inferred extrapolation — trail-running specifics were not in a single snapshot file; derived from the general corpus).

### Ski Mountaineering

- Uphill-focused with skinning or skin-track simulation.
- Transition practice (skins on/off).
- Eccentric quad work for descents.
- Balance of uphill endurance and downhill power.

### Rock Climbing (sport variation)

Climbing has enough sport-specific structure to function as a variation. Core Uphill Athlete principles (periodization, capacity-first, low-intensity base) still apply; the tools are different.

#### ARC Training (Aerobic Restoration & Capillarity)

Low-intensity climbing endurance — the climber's equivalent of Zone 2.

- **Duration:** 20-45 minutes continuous climbing.
- **Intensity:** ~50-60% max effort. Moderate terrain; no acute pump.
- **Frequency:** 2-4x/week during Base Phase or between hard training phases.
- **Mechanism:** Increases forearm capillary density → better oxygen delivery + lactate clearance.
- **Role:** Complementary. Does not replace strength training.

> **Source:** ARC_Training.md.

#### Hangboard Training — phased progression

**Prerequisites** (non-negotiable):
- 6-12 months of regular climbing.
- Ability to perform **5-10 controlled pull-ups**.
- Proper crimp and open-hand technique.
- No active finger, wrist, or elbow injury.

**Schedule:** 2x/week, minimum 48 hours between sessions. 8-12 week macrocycle.

**Phased progression:**
- **Weeks 1-4 (Adaptation):** Longer hangs, more rest. Tendon acclimation.
- **Weeks 5-8 (Strength development):** Gradually add weight; keep rest long.
- **Weeks 9-12 (Strength progression):** Maintain or add weight; reduce rest intervals.

**Primary targets:** Finger flexor tendons (FDP/FDS), forearm flexors, A2/A4 pulley ligaments (the injury-risk zones).

**Safety:** Thorough warm-up, progressive loading, antagonist training (push/extensors).

> **Source:** Base_Phase_Hangboard_Training.md, Advanced_Rock_Climbing_Progression.md.

#### Rock Prodigy 5-phase climbing periodization

A detailed cycle for intermediate/advanced climbers peaking for projects or competitions.

1. **Base Phase (2-4 weeks)** — General strength, ARC training, mobility. Establishes foundational fitness.
2. **Strength Phase (3-4 weeks)** — Max finger strength. Hangboard **2x/week** (dead-hangs, progressive weight); limit bouldering **1-2x/week** (short, powerful, full recovery); general strength maintenance.
3. **Power Phase (3-4 weeks)** — Convert strength to dynamic movement. Campus board (advanced only); limit bouldering; one-arm pull-up progressions and explosive dead-hangs.
4. **Power Endurance Phase (3-4 weeks)** — Sustain high intensity. **4×4s** (4 boulder problems × 4 rounds each, minimal rest); linked circuits at 80-90% max effort; route intervals at redpoint intensity.
5. **Performance / Peaking Phase (2-4 weeks)** — Project attempts; taper volume, maintain intensity; visualization and beta rehearsal.

**Rest:** ≥1 full rest day per week; deload every 4-6 weeks.

> **Source:** Advanced_Rock_Climbing_Progression.md, Training_for_Rock_Climbing_Strength_Endurance_Technique.md.

#### Climbing accessories

- **Weighted pull-ups:** 3-5 reps at 85-90% max load.
- **Antagonist (2x/week):** push-ups, dips, reverse wrist curls — injury prevention.
- **Climbing-specific core:** hanging leg raises, L-sits, plank variations.
- **Movement drills:** silent feet, smearing practice, body positioning, route reading.

> **Source:** Training_for_Rock_Climbing_Strength_Endurance_Technique.md.

## Aging Athlete (variation)

The methodology is the same; the dosing and recovery scale. Core principles (periodization, 80-90% low-intensity, 2x/week strength) still hold.

**Across-the-board adjustments:**
- **Strength:** Keep 2x/week, heavy (3-5 sets × 3-5 reps); add single-leg / stability work (balance, fall risk); avoid excessive volume.
- **Aerobic:** 3-5x/week Zone 1-2, prioritize low-impact (cycling vs. running) as age increases. High-intensity sparingly — 1x/week Zone 3-4 intervals with more recovery time between.
- **Mobility:** Daily 10-15 min (dynamic pre-workout, static post, foam rolling). This is not optional after 40.
- **Recovery:** Deload every 3-4 weeks minimum; longer adaptation cycles. Increase protein to **1.2-1.6 g/kg body weight/day**.

**Decade-specific tuning:**
- **40s-50s:** Maintain strength and aerobic base at current levels; give high-intensity work more recovery time than you did in your 30s.
- **50s-60s:** Shift to joint-friendly modalities (cycling, elliptical, rowing over running); reduce high-impact volume; add rest days.
- **60s-70s+:** Stability, balance, and mobility become as important as endurance; low-impact dominates; strength for functional independence, not performance.

> **Source:** Strength_Mobility_for_Aging_Mountain_Athlete.md.

## Contraindications

- **Wrong sport focus:** This protocol is for mountain endurance athletes. It is not appropriate for pure strength/bodybuilding goals, sprint sports, or rapid-weight-loss regimes.
- **ADS present, intensity too soon:** If HR Drift Test shows a very low AeT or if the athlete has chronic high-intensity history, adding Zone 3-4 before an adequate base will stall or regress progress. Spend 8-16 weeks in Zone 1-2 first.
- **Hangboard prerequisites unmet:** Beginner climbers, anyone with an active finger/wrist/elbow injury, and climbers without the pull-up baseline (5-10 clean reps) should not hangboard. Substitute ARC and general strength.
- **Overtraining risk:** High-volume approach demands diligent recovery. Sustained RHR rise, HRV drop, mood decline, or unexplained fatigue are signs to back off volume and extend the deload.
- **Insufficient base equipment:** A chest-strap HR monitor is required for individualization. Wrist-based monitors are too inaccurate for the HR Drift Test.

## Variations

### Low-Volume (5-7 hrs/week)

For time-constrained athletes.
- 2 key aerobic sessions (1 long, 1 moderate).
- 2 strength sessions.
- 1-2 easy recovery sessions.
- Prioritize the long weekend session above all else.

### High-Volume (12-20 hrs/week)

For serious / competitive mountain athletes.
- Daily aerobic sessions, often doubles.
- 2 strength sessions.
- 1-2 intensity sessions in Build/Peak.
- Requires diligent recovery (sleep, nutrition, monitoring).

### Beginner Entry (3-4 → 6-8 hrs/week)

For athletes new to structured training.
- Start 3-4 hrs/week.
- 3 easy aerobic sessions + 2 gym sessions.
- Build to 6-8 hrs/week over 8-12 weeks before starting a formal periodized cycle.
- Primary goals: develop the habit, learn HR zones, establish baseline AeT via Drift Test.

## Glossary

- **AeT** — Aerobic Threshold. Highest intensity at which the body primarily burns fat; top of Zone 2.
- **AnT** — Anaerobic Threshold. Intensity above which lactate accumulates faster than it can be cleared (≈ MLSS).
- **ADS** — Aerobic Deficiency Syndrome. Descriptive term for an athlete with a weak aerobic base relative to their top end.
- **ARC** — Aerobic Restoration & Capillarity. Low-intensity, sustained climbing for forearm capillary adaptation.
- **ME** — Muscular Endurance. Sustained force production under load; distinct from max strength.
- **MLSS** — Maximum Lactate Steady State. The intensity boundary at AnT.
- **RHR** — Resting Heart Rate. Morning, pre-activity. Sustained rise signals overtraining.
- **HRV** — Heart Rate Variability. Autonomic recovery marker. Sustained drop signals overtraining.
- **KIS** — "Keep It Simple" strength template: 4 movement patterns, 2x/week, year-round.
- **4×4s** — Climbing-specific power-endurance protocol: 4 boulder problems × 4 rounds with minimal rest.
